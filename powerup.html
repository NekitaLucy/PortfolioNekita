Power-Up

Duration of the project: November 2022 - December 2022

Role: Research, ideation, and design.

Level up your productivity and develop positive habits.

Power-Up, the user-friendly habit tracker app, incorporates a unique feature offering three distinct productivity levels. This design allows users to seamlessly balance their to-do lists and add new habits, fostering a balanced and productive lifestyle.

Purpose

To design the UI for the mobile version of a productivity app primarily for 16 to 24-year-olds who want to keep their study plans and to-do lists in one place and manage them on the go.

Problem statement

This project aims to design three critical features for a productivity app that addresses the challenge of finding a balance in changing user habits.

My solution

I chose this idea because I wanted to design a productivity app that lets the user select a limit on how many habits and tasks they want to work on and to motivate the user's goals through an incentive feature.

    These features include:

  • Creating a user profile.
  • Generating task lists.
  • Tracking task completion.

    To develop 2 to 3 key functionalities and design the UI for their mobile app.

  1. Data management
  2. Incentives

Process

Analyze the design of 2 competitor apps.

Competitor Analysis

  1. Habitify
  2. Todoist

For the competitor analysis, I analyzed 2 popular habit and productivity apps to identify evidence of design principles and heuristics and identifying how they come into play in my app. Namely;

  • Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics
  • Gestalt Properties
  • Gestalt Laws of Grouping
  • Principles of Design
  • Gestalt Psychology

Common design patterns in Habitify and Todoist

  • They both have a layout that is divided into 3 columns. The left tab organizes the main tasks/habits and is linked to what the user will see in the center layout (the main page).
  • They both have a plus button to add new habits or tasks.
  • They both have a calendar which is organized by; current day, week, and monthly overview.
  • Feedback: (rewards notification)

Takeaway from the competitor analysis.

Features that I applied to design my productivity app.

  • Create a user profile.
  • Data management to organize and display the user's progress.
  • A calendar overview.
  • The incentive feature when you have completed your goals for the month.
  • Feedback: (rewards notification)

Low-fidelity Wireframes

Sketches of the features on low-fidelity wireframes to get a rough layout idea. Based on the key functionalities for the app I did some quick sketches and ideation for the first set of low-fidelity mobile wireframes to brainstorm the layout and content for each screen. Keeping in mind during the sketching process what a user will need to do to get from point A to B.

Mid-fidelity Wireframes

Grid

Defining a grid in order to refine the designs within each frame and aligning the layou. Creating a framework that designers can use to organize text, graphics, or any other element related to the design for consistency in spacing increments and not let any of the elements end within a gutter.

Layout design and spacing

Applying the layout design and spacing to the grayscale wireframes. Using the existing grayscale shapes, refine the layout of content on your screens. I made annotations on the spacing increments on key elements on each screen.

UI elements and Hierarchy

Adding the remaining UI elements before establishing a visual hierarchy for each of your screens. Creating a visual hierarchy in grayscale. Annotating the wireframes to indicate the thought process behind my visual hierarchy.

Explore, Design, Test icons

Research: other apps and icons to help inspire your icon ideas.

Design: Develop 2 to 3 solutions for each.

Test: I asked my friends and family to provide feedback on my designs. To see if they understood what the icons are trying to say. Does one solution communicate the message better than others? Finally, after re-designing the icons based on the feedback from friends and family, I Incorporated the icons into mid-fidelity wireframes.

Visual Design Inspiration Board

Coming up with the visual direction for the project before adding visual design elements. curating inspirational design material to guide the visual design for the project. Thinking about what kinds of emotions would you like to evoke? What personality do you want to present to your users? Decide on 2-3 keywords that define this “mood.”

Color Palette

I chose a minimalist color background with secondary colours to highlight selected habits and a blue accent colour for active buttons.

Color palette testing

I created two different versions of a color palette before deciding on one and applying them both to the designs.

User testing on the color options.

Finally reaching out to colleagues about the two options to see which style they prefer. could also reach out to friends, family, and online peers to get some feedback on your use of color.

Annotating the dominant, support, and accent colors in your palette.

Typography stylesheet

Based on the tone of the inspiration board I chose a rounded typeface for an approachable and friendly tone.

Complete UI Design

Main features

  • The user can tap the circle to mark the habit as complete, and if it was a mistake, they can tap again.
  • The day's main task is what the user highlights as the main priority.
  • The user can see the tasks that they have yet to complete and the total of tasks they completed.
  • A calendar view of the days the user completed a habit is highlighted, which the user can view on a monthly and yearly summary.

Concludion

Key insights I took away from the project?

When it comes to low-fidelity wireframes, it helps to sketch out a first draft on paper because its the fastest way to sketch out the general layout, then testing that wireframe on a digital platform such as AdobeXD, Sketch or Figma will help with visualizing it and adjusting some placements.
When moving on to the mid-fidelity wireframe and annotating the spacing and layout, the annotation helps as an efficient reminder on the spacing measurements for each frame.
The icon design should have a consistent pattern whether its glyph, line, animated, flat, filled, so that they are recognized as being a family of icons having the same basic functionality.
The visual design board will set the themes and tone of the app by laying out a set of typography styles that is legible on the device, and style wise is related to the theme of the app which in this app was about motivation and an elegant theme. The colour palette should have a complimentary contrast, so that the text or buttons stand out from the background.

If I could do the project again, what would I do differently? I would change how the data management frame looks and look into more inspiration on how to design for a frame about statistics.
